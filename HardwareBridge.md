## 1. The Convergence Claim

The single largest insight the two findings documents produce jointly was visible in neither alone: post-quantum cryptography and inference-anchored consensus are converging on the same arithmetic, and therefore on the same silicon. The post-quantum migration is, at the hardware level, a migration from elliptic-curve big-integer arithmetic to two primitives — parallel hashing and small-prime-field linear algebra (F-B6: FRI and hash-based systems are the production post-quantum path; the lattice schemes of FIPS 203/204 reduce to number-theoretic transforms over small moduli plus Keccak). Inference-anchored proof-of-work independently concluded that its puzzle arithmetic must be integer or finite-field (F-D4), and [TensorZKP](https://eprint.iacr.org/2026/1250) closed the loop by demonstrating that finite-field arithmetic maps onto low-precision tensor units at a 36x advantage over conventional GPU code paths. Three workload families that had separate hardware histories — neural-network inference, consensus puzzles, and cryptographic proof generation — now all want the same block: a dense low-precision multiply-accumulate array over small integers, fed from nearby memory.

The convergence has a direction. Inference volume dwarfs the other two workloads and drives the silicon roadmap; the cryptographic workloads are adapting themselves to inference hardware, not the reverse. This is the hardware-side restatement of Topic D's ASIC inversion: where cryptography once demanded its own silicon (SHA-256 ASICs, MSM/NTT accelerators for pairing SNARKs), the post-quantum and post-EC generation is being *designed to borrow* the AI fleet. The stranded-asset list is equally concrete: elliptic-curve point-multiplication blocks, pairing accelerators, and the multi-scalar-multiplication pipelines built for Groth16-era proving (F-B6's stranded stack, in silicon form).

## 2. Workload-to-Silicon Map

The table maps each algorithmic conclusion to the kernel it implies and the hardware block that serves it; the findings column anchors every row to graded evidence.

| Algorithmic insight | Kernel it implies | Hardware block | Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|
| FRI/STARK proving is the post-quantum ZK path | Merkle hashing plus small-field NTT and low-degree extension | Wide hash engines; small-prime MAC arrays (31-bit and smaller moduli) | F-B6 |
| Lattice signatures and KEMs standardize | NTT over 12–23-bit moduli, Keccak sponge | Vectorized modular multiply; Keccak permutation unit | F-B1, F-B2 |
| Signature aggregation is load-bearing for block space | Recursive SNARK proving of hash-based signatures | Throughput prover: the same small-field MAC arrays, batch-oriented | F-B3 |
| Consensus puzzle arithmetic must be integer or finite-field | INT8-input, INT32-accumulate GEMM | Existing tensor cores; determinism spec needed | F-D4, F-D11 |
| ZK proving itself maps to tensor units | Finite-field GEMM on low-precision MMA instructions | Tensor cores as finite-field engines | F-D3, TensorZKP |
| Verification must stay node-cheap | Merkle path checks, single hash chains | Latency-bound scalar and cache path — commodity CPUs suffice | F-B7, F-D3 |
| Provenance, not verification, is the open problem | Attested workload binding | GPU trusted execution (confidential computing) as an alternative trust root | F-D9 |

Two asymmetries in the map matter for design. Proving and puzzle-solving are throughput workloads that want the tensor path and tolerate deep pipelines; verification is a latency workload that must stay cheap on unspecialized machines, because the moment full-node verification needs an accelerator, the decentralization argument collapses. And the hash engines split by context: Keccak and BLAKE-family hashing is bit-logic that vectorizes on any substrate, while [Poseidon2](https://www.poseidon-initiative.info/)-class arithmetic hashes are field arithmetic — they belong to the MAC-array world, which is exactly why Ethereum's [$1M Poseidon Prize](https://www.theblock.co/post/386938/ethereum-foundation-forms-post-quantum-security-team-adds-1-million-research-prize) targets confidence in a hash that proving hardware can afford.

## 3. Design Implications by Hardware Block

**Small-field MAC arrays are the new universal primitive.** The circle-STARK provers ([Stwo](https://github.com/starkware-libs/stwo) over the Mersenne-31 field, [Plonky3](https://github.com/Plonky3/Plonky3) and [RISC Zero](https://github.com/risc0/risc0) over 31-bit friendly primes) chose their fields to fit 32-bit lanes; TensorZKP's contribution is field arithmetic shaped to fit *tensor* lanes. The design question for a compiled hardware block is whether to build a native small-prime MAC — modular reduction folded into the accumulate, eliminating the correction arithmetic that consumes TensorZKP's overhead — or to keep borrowing INT8/INT32 units with software reduction. A native block wins exactly where Topic E's premise points: embedded-memory designs where weights or twiddle factors sit in SRAM or ROM beside the array. The same array serves STARK proving, lattice NTTs, and integer consensus puzzles; that triple duty is what justifies the silicon area in a way no single cryptographic workload ever did.

**Determinism should be specified, not discovered.** The program's determinism thread — floating-point tensor paths non-reproducible across vendors at instruction level, integer paths exact by associativity, batch-invariant kernels proving per-implementation floating-point determinism is achievable (F-D4, F-D11) — resolves into one hardware-facing demand: a vendor-neutral deterministic tensor profile, an IEEE-754-analogue for matrix units specifying exact integer accumulate behavior, reduction-order independence, and a conformance test. Every workload in the map benefits: consensus puzzles get cross-vendor validity, verifiable-inference schemes get exactness instead of [TOPLOC](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2501.16007)-style tolerance bands, and reproducible-inference systems get their batch invariance guaranteed below the kernel layer instead of enforced above it. Dataflow architectures ([Groq](https://groq.com)'s compile-time-scheduled SRAM design, and wafer-scale relatives) deserve specific attention here: static scheduling makes their execution order fixed by construction, so they offer bitwise determinism as an architectural property rather than a kernel discipline — a consensus-grade virtue their designers built for entirely different reasons [Modeled; conformance across compiler versions is the untested assumption].

**Memory hierarchy decides decentralization.** Topic A's capacity-versus-bandwidth vocabulary reappears here as a design dial rather than a defense: an integer-tensor puzzle whose working set fits consumer GPU SRAM and VRAM keeps the mining base broad, while one sized to pod-scale HBM hands the network to hyperscaler fleets (F-D6). Puzzle parameterization is therefore hardware policy — the matrix dimensions and operand residency of the consensus kernel are a decentralization decision disguised as a performance decision, and they should track the *consumer* end of the [microscaling-format](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.10537) roadmap, not the datacenter end. The same logic runs the other way for the post-quantum stack: F-B2's signature blowup is a bandwidth and storage tax on every full node, and SNARK aggregation (F-B3) is best understood as trading node bandwidth for prover compute — moving cost from the hardware everyone must own to hardware someone must own, which is a centralization trade the block-space debate has not priced explicitly.

**Attestation is the hardware answer to the provenance gap.** F-D9 concluded that no cryptographic audit binds a matrix multiplication to genuine demand, since operand statistics are adversarially forgeable. GPU trusted execution environments — confidential-computing modes on current datacenter parts — offer a different trust root: the customer's workload is signed into an enclave, and the attestation, not the operand distribution, proves the work was requested. This converts Topic D's open provenance problem into a hardware-trust problem with known costs (enclave overhead, vendor root-of-trust concentration) and a known failure mode: it re-centralizes trust in the attestation keyholder, which for a permissionless chain may be disqualifying. It is recorded here as the only currently buildable provenance mechanism, not as an endorsement [Speculative on suitability; the mechanism itself is shipping].

**Verification hardware must stay boring.** Every design above concerns the prover and miner side. The complementary constraint is easy to lose: F-B7 and the verification rows of the map say the validating node's hardware profile should remain a commodity CPU with ordinary caches — Merkle paths, hash chains, a few field operations. Any proposal whose *verifier* needs a tensor unit has moved the accelerator requirement from the optional role to the mandatory one and should be rejected on that ground alone, whatever its prover-side elegance.

## 4. Bridge Findings

Cross-topic findings carry F-X identifiers and cite their parent findings; most consequential first.

**F-X1.** Post-quantum cryptography and inference-anchored consensus converge on one hardware primitive — dense low-precision integer and small-prime-field multiply-accumulate with adjacent memory — making tensor-class silicon the shared substrate of inference, proving, and puzzle-solving. The cryptographic workloads adapt to the AI roadmap, not the reverse [Modeled from F-B6, F-D4, and the TensorZKP result].

**F-X2.** A vendor-neutral deterministic tensor profile is the highest-leverage unbuilt artifact in the combined space: it de-risks consensus puzzles, upgrades verifiable inference from tolerance to exactness, and standardizes what batch-invariant kernels currently enforce by hand. No standards body owns it, and its absence is the residual reason F-D4's cross-vendor claim is Modeled rather than Measured [Modeled from F-D4, F-D11].

**F-X3.** Statically scheduled dataflow architectures provide bitwise determinism as an architectural property, making them consensus-eligible for floating-point workloads that GPUs can only serve through pinned-kernel monoculture (F-D11). The decentralization price is that such hardware is scarcer than GPUs; the finding identifies an eligibility, not a recommendation [Modeled].

**F-X4.** Consensus-kernel parameterization is decentralization policy: working-set size selects the mining hardware class, so the F-D6 hyperscaler exposure is partly a tunable, not a fate. The tuning discipline — track consumer-tier memory and format roadmaps — is the same discipline Topic E prescribes for embedded-memory model design, applied to a puzzle [Modeled from F-D6].

**F-X5.** SNARK aggregation relocates cost from universal node bandwidth to concentrated prover compute; the post-quantum block-space fix and the inference-hardware convergence are therefore the same event viewed from two sides — aggregation provers will run on the tensor-class substrate of F-X1, and whoever operates them becomes infrastructure. The block-space debate should account for this transfer explicitly [Modeled from F-B2, F-B3].

**F-X6.** GPU trusted execution is the only currently shipping mechanism that closes the F-D9 provenance gap, and it does so by re-centralizing trust in a vendor attestation root — acceptable for verifiable-compute markets, unresolved for permissionless consensus. The gap between those two acceptability judgments is a concrete research boundary [Speculative on the consensus side].

**F-X7.** Puzzle hardness is decided at the algorithm-data-structure boundary, not in silicon or in proofs alone: Equihash's ASIC resistance was collapsed by a data-structure substitution — the index-pointer representation fixed memory-access patterns and per-merge bounds, cutting peak memory to roughly 49 MB at Zcash parameters and handing the advantage to multi-chip ASICs (Tang–Sun–Gong, [eprint 2025/1351](https://eprint.iacr.org/2025/1351); Bai et al., NDSS 2019) — and it is restored the same way, by a constraint (Requihash's regularity binding) that renders the hostile data structure inapplicable and makes index pointers cost double rather than less. Hardware-anchored designs from any topic in this program should be audited for their hostile-data-structure surface, not only their arithmetic and memory totals [Reported].

## 5. Consequences for the Program

The bridge changes three things in the plan's remaining work. Topic E's survey (`HardwareNative.md`) inherits sharpened questions: its numerics axis should evaluate ternary and log-number representations not only for inference economics but for whether they preserve the finite-field mapping that F-X1 depends on, and its architecture axis should treat static-scheduling determinism (F-X3) as a first-class evaluation criterion alongside bytes-per-token. The Stage 4 prototype gains a second purpose at no added scope: the cross-vendor INT8 bit-exactness sweep already planned as the F-D4 residual test is simultaneously the feasibility floor for the deterministic tensor profile of F-X2, so its pass/fail matrix should be written up as a draft conformance table, not just a finding upgrade. And the synthesis document should treat F-X1 as a candidate organizing thesis: the program began with six topics connected by dependency arrows, and the bridge suggests they are more tightly coupled than the plan drew them — one substrate, with consensus, cryptography, and models as tenants negotiating for the same multiply-accumulate area.

Business and social consequences follow the same lines and are recorded for the synthesis rather than developed here. The monetizable surfaces are the conformance-and-attestation toolchain (F-X2, F-X6) and aggregation-prover operation (F-X5), both of which earn revenue regardless of which chain or model family wins. The social exposure is the F-X1 coupling itself: when consensus security, cryptographic migration, and AI capability all draw on one silicon supply chain, a shock to that supply chain — export controls, fab concentration, a demand spike in any tenant — propagates to all three, and no current policy framework models them jointly.
